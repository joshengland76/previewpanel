How we validated the PreviewPanel score

A plain explanation of the study behind the prediction — what we did, how we checked ourselves, and what it can't tell you.

What we did

We recruited 259 creators across 19 content niches — cooking, fitness, comedy, travel, and 15 others — and collected their TikTok videos. Before we ever looked at how those videos actually performed, our judging panel scored each one on hook strength, emotional pull, pacing, clarity, and related factors. Only after scoring was locked in did we track each video's real 30-day engagement: likes, shares, and saves per view. The prediction model was built from that pairing — judged qualities on one side, real outcomes on the other.

How we kept ourselves honest

The model was tested only on creators it had never seen during training — a stricter bar than testing on held-out videos from creators it already knew. Before any of that testing began, we set aside a sealed group of 30 creators — a "lockbox" — and didn't look at their data until every modeling decision was final. We opened it exactly once. Every analysis choice along the way was written down and dated before we saw its result, not after; that log grew to 21 entries. And every video is scored against its own creator's typical performance, not the whole platform's — so one creator with an unusually large following can't skew what "good" looks like for everyone else.

What we found

In that held-out lockbox test, videos the model ranked in its top tier beat the creator's own typical engagement about 2 out of 3 times — well ahead of a coin flip. That result held up reliably in 16 of the 19 niches we studied. For the remaining 3 (Gaming, Educational/How-To, and Dancing), we don't yet trust the numeric score enough to show it — you'll see qualitative feedback there instead, while we keep collecting data.

What it can't do

This is not a virality predictor. It cannot tell you whether a specific video will take off, and it makes no promises about any single outcome. The patterns it's built on are correlational — things that tend to go together with stronger performance — not causal instructions; it doesn't tell you to make a video longer or shorter, for instance, just because duration is one of the things it accounts for. And so far, this model has only been validated against the historical study data described above — validating it against real PreviewPanel users' own posted videos is ongoing work, not yet complete.
